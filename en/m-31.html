Andromeda Galaxy

Posted on 5-10-2025 • Author: Ziugpro

Andromeda (M-31) is a spiral galaxy located in the constellation Andromeda in the northern sky near the constellation Pegasus. It is the closest spiral galaxy to our Milky Way, about 2.5 million light-years away.

Name: M-31, NGC 224, UGC 454, PGC 2557

The Andromeda Galaxy was once considered the largest galaxy in the Local Group, which includes the Andromeda Galaxy, the Milky Way, the Triangulum Galaxy (in the constellation Triangulum), and about 30 other smaller galaxies. Due to recent discoveries based on advanced measurement methods and new data, scientists now believe that the Milky Way contains more dark matter than Andromeda and may be the most massive galaxy in the Local Group. However, recent observations from the Spitzer Space Telescope indicate that M31 contains approximately one trillion (10¹²) stars, far surpassing the number of stars in the Milky Way. Estimates from 2006 suggest that the Milky Way’s mass is about ~80% of Andromeda’s mass, or roughly 7.1×10¹¹ times the mass of the Sun. We can easily see the Andromeda Galaxy with the naked eye in sparsely populated areas that are less affected by dust and light pollution, unlike in urban environments. M31 appears relatively small to the naked eye because only its bright core is visible, but in reality, the galaxy’s angular diameter is seven times that of the full Moon. M31 is considered the most distant object visible to the naked eye after the Triangulum Galaxy. Within the Local Group, the Andromeda Galaxy is the largest galaxy, followed by the Milky Way as the second largest, and the Triangulum Galaxy as the third largest
